Eugene

President of Workers

1. Identity and Demographics (The Basics)

Full Name and Nicknames

Eugene. He is universally known as the "President" of Workers (Ilhae).

Age and Birthdate

Introduced as a high school student, operating in the 18–19 age range.

Nationality and Ethnicity

Korean.

Occupation and Income

President of Workers and CEO of its various legal corporate fronts (entertainment agencies, private security, etc.). His income is astronomical, generating billions of won through both legal business ventures and horrific illegal operations (drug trafficking, illegal streaming, kidnapping).

Current Location and Home Type

Seoul, South Korea. He operates out of highly secure, pristine corporate boardrooms and luxury penthouses, surrounded by layers of elite bodyguards.

Education and Language

A high school student, but his true education is in corporate warfare, economics, and psychological manipulation. He speaks with the vocabulary of a seasoned, middle-aged corporate magnate.

2. Physical Presence (The Surface)

Body Build and Height

Short, slender, and physically frail compared to the towering martial artists around him. He possesses absolutely no combat ability.

Facial Features

He has a soft, unthreatening face with neatly parted hair. His eyes are sharp and calculating, usually magnified behind a pair of round, intellectual glasses.

Distinguishing Marks

Unlike the other crew heads who are covered in battle scars, Eugene’s defining physical trait is his complete lack of them. His pristine physical state highlights how successfully he has insulated himself behind his fighters.

Clothing and Fashion

He weaponizes his appearance. He almost exclusively wears his neatly pressed high school uniform or sharp, understated business suits. It is a deliberate aesthetic designed to make him look like a harmless, polite student, disarming his enemies.

Sensory Details

He gives off the sterile, perfectly temperature-controlled aura of a corporate office. His voice is incredibly calm, polite, and unhurried—even when the room is being torn apart by superhumans.

Health and Fitness

Physically weak. He relies entirely on his intellect and his bodyguards for survival.

3. Psychology and Personality (The Internal)

Core Personality Traits

Utilitarian, intensely polite, sociopathic, hyper-rational, and fiercely protective of his in-group.

The "Wound" and "Lie"

The Wound: The severe bullying and marginalization he and his twin brother, Yuseong, endured as children. Society deemed them weak—Eugene for his body, and Yuseong for his severe neurodivergence.

The Lie: The absolute conviction that human life is merely a commodity, and that hoarding infinite wealth and systemic power is the only way to create a safe world for his brother.

Greatest Fears and Regrets

His one true vulnerability is his twin brother, Yuseong. His greatest fear is seeing Yuseong hurt, or losing the carefully constructed empire that keeps them both safe from the cruelty of the world.

Strengths and Weaknesses

His strength is his genius-level intellect, strategic foresight, and ability to buy or blackmail anyone. His weakness is his total physical vulnerability and his emotional blind spot regarding his brother.

Moral Compass and Beliefs

Pragmatic evil. He justifies horrific crimes against teenagers, runaways, and streamers because he views the world strictly through a spreadsheet of profit and loss. If an action secures his empire, it is morally justified in his eyes.

Default Mood

Cheerful, polite condescension.

4. Backstory and History (The Past)

Family Dynamics

The twin brother of Yuseong (the VVIP of Workers). They are two halves of a whole: Eugene is the unstoppable brain, and Yuseong is the unstoppable brawn. Their bond is the only genuine, unconditional love Eugene experiences.

Early Life Events

Realizing early on that physical strength has limits, but money and systemic power do not. He watched his brother be treated like a monster or a victim, and decided to build a world where Yuseong would be treated like a king.

Pivotal Life Moments

1. Forming Workers and establishing its multi-affiliate corporate structure.
2. Catching the eye of Charles Choi (Elite) and Gun, proving that a crew could generate corporate-level wealth.
3. His realization that to truly be safe, he must eventually betray and overthrow Charles Choi.

Job and Relationship History

He treats relationships strictly as transactions. He recruits absolute monsters (like Tom Lee, Mandeok Bang, and Eli Jang) by finding their exact price—whether that price is money, protection, or leverage.

5. Behaviors and Mannerisms (The Expression)

Speech Patterns

Overwhelmingly formal. He uses honorifics with everyone, even people he is about to have killed. He addresses other gang leaders as "President" or "Sir."

Habits and Quirks

Pushing up his glasses by the bridge when analyzing a situation.

Assigning a literal monetary value to people's combat strength (e.g., "You possess a combat power worth 10 billion won").

Drinking tea or sipping coffee while watching horrific, blood-splattering fights from a safe distance.

Typical Reactions

When physically threatened, he doesn't flinch or beg. He simply smiles, knowing that his "shields" (Mandeok or Yuseong) will intercept the blow before it lands. He treats physical violence as a math problem he has already solved.

Hobbies and Interests

Corporate acquisitions, reading, and ensuring his brother is happy and entertained.

6. Story Role and Dynamics (The Function)

Core Motivation

To destroy Charles Choi, monopolize the underworld, and build an unassailable fortress of wealth so that nobody can ever look down on him or Yuseong again.

External Goals vs. Internal Needs

Goal: Crush Allied, absorb the other Major Crews, and eliminate the HNH Group.

Need: He desperately needs to maintain the illusion of absolute control, because if the system falls, he and his brother return to being helpless victims.

The Character Arc

Eugene is the ultimate "final boss" of the crew-head era. His arc is about the limits of intellect and capital against the chaotic, unpredictable nature of human willpower (represented by Daniel Park and Allied).

Key Relationships

Yuseong: His twin brother, his heart, and his ultimate weapon.

Mandeok Bang: His loyal vice-president and primary shield.

Charles Choi: His former benefactor turned ultimate rival. They are playing a high-stakes game of 4D chess against each other.

Secrets

He holds the key to Charles Choi's past weaknesses and the secrets behind the two bodies. His mind is a vault of blackmail material on every major figure in South Korea.

"Eugene proves that in a world of superhuman fighters, the most dangerous person is often the one holding the checkbook."

Part I: The Core Lessons
(What We Learn from Eugene)

1. The Supremacy of Systems Over Brute Force (The Leverage Principle)

Eugene’s foundational philosophy is that individual physical effort—no matter how grueling or disciplined—has a hard biological limit. You can only lift so much weight, strike so hard, or endure so much physical punishment before the body breaks down. Eugene bypasses this biological ceiling entirely by building systems.

The Lesson: Hard work and physical discipline are noble, but true, unassailable power comes from building structures that operate independently of your physical presence. Eugene didn't train his body to fight a hundred men; he built a corporate structure (Workers) that employs a thousand men to fight for him while he safely reviews the profit margins.

Application: Whether you are writing lines of code to automate a tedious task, building a program to fetch and filter opportunities across the web, or structuring a business, the goal is to create a "machine" that works for you 24/7. Eugene proves that the architect of the system will always hold authority over the strongest cog within it.

2. Information as the Ultimate Currency (The Radar Strategy)

Eugene never enters a conflict blindly. He operates a massive intelligence network that tracks the movements, finances, psychological weaknesses, and interpersonal relationships of every key player in Seoul.

The Lesson: Wars are won in the data collection phase. Eugene defeated legendary fighters not by out-punching them, but by knowing exactly what they valued most and holding it hostage. He calculates an opponent's "combat power" and assigns them a literal monetary value, turning chaotic human conflict into a predictable spreadsheet.

Application: Before launching an offensive—whether in business, negotiations, or competitive projects—you must map the terrain. Building your own "radar" to continuously gather intelligence, track market trends, or monitor competitors ensures you are never surprised. If you know an opponent's inputs and constraints, you can predict their outputs.

3. Flawless Delegation and Talent Acquisition

Because Eugene acknowledges his absolute physical vulnerability, he is obsessed with talent acquisition. He surrounds himself with "shields"—anomalies of physical violence like Mandeok Bang, Tom Lee, and his twin brother, Yuseong.

The Lesson: A true mastermind does not need to be good at everything; they only need to be good at finding the people who are. Eugene identifies the exact price of a monster. For Tom Lee, it is exorbitant amounts of money. For Eli Jang, it is the safety of his family. For Mandeok, it is respect and loyalty.

Application: Recognize your own weak points with cold, objective clarity. If you are the visionary or the strategist, do not waste time trying to become the brawler. Instead, learn how to identify, recruit, and properly compensate specialists who can execute the tasks you cannot.

4. Emotional Insulation and High-Pressure Composure

One of Eugene’s most chilling traits is his physical reaction to violence. When a room is being torn apart by high-level martial artists, walls are caving in, and blood is spilling inches from his face, Eugene routinely adjusts his glasses, sips his tea, and speaks in a polite, unhurried monotone.

The Lesson: Panic is a tax on intellect. Eugene maintains his composure because he has already mentally modeled the worst-case scenario. He trusts the structural integrity of his defenses (his bodyguards and his systemic leverage) so completely that immediate, visceral threats do not trigger his fight-or-flight response.

Application: True discipline is not just physical endurance, like enduring a massive daily commute or a heavy training block; it is the mental endurance to remain analytical when everything around you is chaotic. By treating high-pressure situations as mathematical equations rather than emotional crises, you retain access to your higher reasoning.

Part II: The Fatal Flaws
(What to Actively Avoid)

To fully learn from Eugene, we must also dissect the flaws that lead to the systemic cracks in his empire.

The Spreadsheet Fallacy

Eugene’s greatest intellectual failing is his belief that human beings are entirely rational and quantifiable. He reduces people to code, metrics, and combat numbers. He fails to account for the "X-factor" of human willpower—the irrational, illogical grit that allows a broken fighter to stand back up purely out of spite or love.

The Trap: Systems fail when they cannot process the sheer, unquantifiable weight of human resolve.

The Blind Spot of Absolute Love

Eugene is a sociopathic pragmatist with everyone except his twin brother, Yuseong. Yuseong is Eugene’s heart. When Yuseong is threatened or hurt, Eugene’s flawless logic crumbles into desperate, reactive emotion.

The Trap: It proves that no matter how insulated or robotic a person attempts to make themselves, a single emotional tether can be pulled to unravel the entire machine.

The Arrogance of the Ivory Tower

Because he is so far removed from the physical toll of his decisions, Eugene frequently underestimates the boiling resentment of the people at the bottom of his hierarchy. He treats the suffering of his affiliates as a necessary business expense, forgetting that a cornered, desperate workforce will eventually turn on the architect.

"Eugene teaches us that a sharp mind, heavily optimized systems, and ruthless pragmatism can conquer physical titans. However, he also serves as a warning: if you build an empire that treats humanity merely as data points to be exploited, you will eventually be torn down by the very human spirit you failed to quantify."
